01 — Project overview

Improving shared spending with smarter budget visibility

iOS redesign Expense sharing UX/UI Figma Product thinking

This project is a focused Tricount iOS feature enhancement rather than a full product redesign. I kept the original app structure and introduced a spend limit feature with a supporting summary section to help users understand how much they have spent, how much is left, and how their expenses evolve over time.


The concept was based on review analysis, gap analysis, and competitor observation. The goal was to improve budget awareness without disrupting Tricount's existing shared-expense flow.

02 — Challenge

A useful expense app, but limited budget feedback

Tricount already helps users split expenses and understand balances, but the experience can do more to support spending awareness during a trip or shared activity. Users can log expenses easily, yet they may still need extra mental effort to understand whether they are staying within a planned budget.


Instead of redesigning the whole app, I focused on one clear product opportunity: adding a spend limit and pairing it with a concise summary that makes budget status visible at a glance.

04 — Added features

Two small additions with a bigger planning benefit

Spend limit

A single optional input added to the tricount creation flow lets users define a group budget before any expenses are logged. It integrates cleanly with the existing form without adding friction.

  • Added as an optional field in the creation form
  • Compatible with any currency Tricount supports
  • Unlocks the summary section once a limit is set
  • Zero disruption to users who skip it

Spending summary

A new collapsible section at the top of the tricount detail view gives users a clear picture of their budget status without requiring them to do any mental math.

  • Shows total spent vs. total limit at a glance
  • Displays remaining budget with a progress indicator
  • Includes a simple timeline of daily expense activity
  • Collapses to stay out of the way when not needed

05 — Process

How the concept was shaped

01 — Review analysis

I read through App Store reviews for Tricount and competing expense apps to find recurring frustrations. Budget awareness and lack of spending feedback appeared consistently across user comments.

02 — Gap analysis

Mapping Tricount's current feature set against what users expressed needing made the gap clear: the app tracks what was spent but provides no context for whether that amount is within any kind of plan.

03 — Competitor observation

Apps like Splitwise and Spendee handle group budgets and spending summaries in different ways. Observing their approaches helped me define what a minimal, non-intrusive version of this feature could look like inside Tricount.

04 — Concept & wireframing

I sketched how the spend limit input and summary section could sit inside the existing flow before moving into Figma. The priority was making both additions feel like they had always been there, not like bolt-ons.

05 — Figma prototyping

The 10 portfolio frames were built in Figma using Tricount's existing visual language — same type scale, same component rhythm — so the new screens integrate seamlessly with the original app design.
